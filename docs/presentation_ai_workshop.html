The Opportunity

AI tools that accelerate decisions, not replace experts

The goal is faster collaboration, not automation

  • Experts follow repeatable analysis patterns that can be decomposed into steps
  • AI handles the research and computation -- humans make the decisions
  • The result: faster team alignment with transparent, editable assumptions
  • This framework applies to any domain -- pharma BD is today's example
Not replacing the analyst. Giving them a first draft in 2 minutes instead of 2 weeks -- so the team spends time debating strategy, not gathering data.

01

Design Principle 1/5

Decompose the problem

Don't ask one AI to do everything

The single-prompt trap

One massive prompt tries to handle science, market sizing, comparators, and deal pricing all at once.

Result: mediocre at everything, good at nothing.

Specialized agents

Each agent has ONE clear job and does it well.

Like an assembly line vs. one person doing everything.

Science Agent

What is this drug?

→

Market Agent

How big is the opportunity?

→

Synthesizer

Who will pay, and how much?

02

Design Principle 2/5

Define clear ownership

Each agent should know exactly what it owns -- and what it doesn't

Science Agent

Owns the asset's intrinsic properties

Mechanism, clinical data, regulatory status, trial design

Market Agent

Owns the competitive environment

Comparators, market size, standard of care, pricing benchmarks

Synthesizer

Owns the buyer's perspective

Strategic fit, urgency, deal structure, valuation range

Why this matters most: If there's confusion about who owns what, your prompts will be confused too. This is the single most important design decision you'll make.
03

Design Principle 3/5

Let code do math, let AI do reasoning

BD decisions are fast and high-stakes -- you need numbers you can trust in hours, not weeks

Example: Probability of Success

The AI's job is to identify signals:

  • "This is an orphan drug" (+7%)
  • "Has breakthrough designation" (+5%)
  • "Strong Phase 2 data" (+10%)

Then code does the calculation:

13% base + 7% + 5% + 10% = 35%

The Rule

AI extracts and reasons.
Python calculates.

Think of it like a doctor and a lab: the doctor interprets the symptoms, the lab runs the numbers.

This principle alone eliminates 50% of "the AI got the wrong number" complaints.

04

Design Principle 4/5

Show your work

Users don't trust a black box

Every number traces back to a specific assumption:

COMPARATOR

"We identified Scemblix as the standard of care because it has the highest efficacy in the same patient population."

PROBABILITY OF SUCCESS

"Base 13%, adjusted to 32% because of these 5 clinical signals."

DEAL PRICE

"$7B = $3.5B peak sales x 2.0x deal multiple"

The Dual-View Pattern

Show two numbers side by side:

Risk-adjusted

$2.2B

Your investment decision

If-succeed

$7.0B

What buyers negotiate on

Users can edit ANY assumption and recalculate instantly.

05

Design Principle 5/5

Ground in real data, not vibes

If you inject a number into a prompt, the AI will echo it back

The anchoring problem

Telling the AI: "Deals in this space are typically $2-5B"

Result: the AI will ALWAYS output something between $2-5B, regardless of the actual asset.

You've turned your AI into an expensive echo chamber.

Comparable reasoning

Give it raw comparable transactions and let it reason about similarity.

The AI assesses: "How similar is this asset to each comp?" -- a reasoning task, not a lookup.

Our approach: We use 27 real 2025-2026 M&A deals as reference points. The AI evaluates similarity across mechanism, stage, indication, and competitive dynamics. The data speaks; the AI interprets.

Why describe the UI to AI?

You're not writing CSS — you're describing what the user sees and does. The AI translates that into React + Tailwind.

  • Describe the layout structure (sidebar + main area)
  • Describe each component's purpose (not its pixels)
  • Describe the data shape it consumes from the API
  • Describe interactions (click to edit, submit to recalculate)

Two numbers, two purposes

Risk-adjusted value
For your internal investment committee. Accounts for the probability the drug never makes it.

If-succeed value
The price a buyer negotiates on. This is the number that shows up in term sheets.

The gap between the two numbers tells you how much risk the buyer is absorbing.

Vibe Coding

Prompt 3: Validate against real deals

The prompt tests your system against known deal prices — without hardcoding answers

4 blind test cases

  • TERN-701 — displacement archetype (Merck $6.7B)
  • Centessa ORX750 — category creation + CVR ($7.8B)
  • Akero/Novo — emerging TA, MASH ($5.2B)
  • Chimerix/Jazz — rare disease guard ($935M)

Each test specifies what the agent should discover on its own and pass/fail criteria.

What validation catches

  • Wrong comparator discovered → search query too broad
  • Wrong differentiation → rubric thresholds off
  • Deal price too low → deal multiple or revenue multiplier needs tuning
  • Deal price too high → displacement capture rate too aggressive
  • No CVR detected → platform detection too strict

Target: Strategic if-succeed within 30% of actual deal price

Key Takeaways

Five principles for building AI decision tools

These work for any domain, not just pharma

  • Decompose -- One agent, one job. Don't ask a single AI to do everything.
  • Own -- Clear boundaries prevent prompt confusion. Define who owns what.
  • Compute -- AI reasons, code calculates. Never ask AI to do arithmetic.
  • Transparency -- Show every assumption. Make it editable. Trust comes from inspectability.
  • Ground -- Real data beats hardcoded ranges. Let the AI reason over evidence, not echo your biases.
The meta-lesson: The hardest part of building AI tools isn't the technology -- it's the design thinking. Knowing your domain well enough to decompose it is the real superpower.
